English A
Develops oral and written language skills through a variety of strategies. English A focuses on Understanding — decoding and interpreting messages — and Expression — conveying meaning through appropriate language. It is assessed across three external and school-based papers.
A
Grammar & Mechanics
Discourse Type (a) — Foundation of all language skills
▼

The foundational layer of the syllabus. Covers how word choice, grammar, syntax, punctuation, and paragraphing work together to convey meaning — both in reading and writing. Understanding grammar enables students to produce accurate, effective communication.

Understanding
  • Explain meaning conveyed through word choice and grammar
  • Identify effective use of adjectives, word combinations, and unusual phrases
  • Recognise the effect of punctuation marks on meaning
  • Understand how paragraphing creates emphasis and aids comprehension
Expression
  • Use appropriate diction and grammatical forms in speaking and writing
  • Apply suitable punctuation and paragraphing to convey meaning clearly
  • Match word choice and style to purpose, situation, and audience
  • Use correct sentence functions: statements, questions, commands
Key Concepts
Syntax & morphology Formal vs informal register Synonyms & antonyms Punctuation marks & their effects Topic sentences & paragraphing Diction & word choice Caribbean Standard English (CSE) Figurative vs literal language
A
Informative Discourse
Discourse Type (b) — Comprehension, summary & functional writing
▼

Deals with extracting and communicating factual information. Students learn to read and interpret texts for explicit and implied meaning, draw conclusions, interpret visual data, and produce functional written forms such as summaries, letters, reports, and notices.

Understanding
  • Recognise facts stated explicitly in a text
  • Extract specific and implied information from reading or listening
  • Draw valid conclusions and inferences from information presented
  • Identify stated or implied time sequence (e.g. flashbacks)
  • Recognise cause-and-effect relationships
  • Identify main and subordinate ideas and trace their development
  • Interpret tables, charts, diagrams, maps, and cartoons
Expression
  • Communicate factual information clearly and concisely
  • Write well-structured summaries (Paper 02 Section A)
  • Produce functional forms: emails, letters, reports, notices, articles
  • Give clear oral and written instructions and directions
  • Write reports using appropriate tone and format
Key Skills & Text Types
Summarising Paraphrasing Note-taking Report writing Letter writing (formal/informal) Email format Notice writing Reading between the lines Interpreting visual data Cause & effect
A
Literary Discourse
Discourse Type (c) — Appreciating & responding to literature
▼

Develops students' ability to engage with and respond to literary texts — prose, poetry, and drama. Students practise literary analysis, recognise writer's craft, identify themes and devices, and produce imaginative writing of their own, including the short story.

Understanding
  • Recognise and respond to the writer's craft and technique
  • Detect connotations and distinguish denotative/connotative meaning
  • Recognise literary devices: pun, irony, symbolism, exaggeration, innuendo
  • Appreciate appropriateness of tone, mood, register, code, and style
  • Visualise setting, atmosphere, and character in drama
  • Recognise implicit themes and make inferences
  • Respond critically to West Indian and other literature in English
Expression
  • Write a well-crafted short story (Paper 02 Section C)
  • Organise and sequence ideas imaginatively
  • Use language to create mood, atmosphere, and voice
  • Make critical appraisals of values expressed in literature
  • Relate literary themes to everyday living
Key Literary Devices
Simile & metaphor Personification Irony & sarcasm Symbolism Tone & mood Theme Imagery Narrative voice Conflict & resolution Caribbean literature
A
Argumentative Discourse
Discourse Type (d) — Critical thinking & persuasive writing
▼

Trains students to detect, evaluate, and produce persuasive language. Students analyse the techniques used in advertising, speeches, editorials, and debates — then apply them to write their own clear, cogent arguments in the form of essays, speeches, or letters.

Understanding
  • Distinguish factual statements from unsupported opinions
  • Detect bias, perspective, and connotations in language
  • Evaluate the effectiveness of persuasive language devices
  • Recognise techniques of persuasion in media and social intercourse
  • Assess argumentative effects of rhetorical devices
Expression
  • Present reasoned evaluative comments on proposals and situations
  • Employ argumentative techniques for emotional impact
  • Present a logical argument using justifiable techniques
  • Write argumentative essays, speeches, or letters to the editor (Paper 02 Section D)
Rhetorical Devices & Techniques
Rhetorical questions Repetition Hyperbole Irony & sarcasm Paradox & oxymoron Emotive language Appeals to authority Use of statistics Fact vs opinion Bias & perspective
FORMAT
English A — Examination Structure
Papers, timing, and mark breakdown
▼

English A is assessed across three components. Profile Dimension 1 tests Understanding and Profile Dimension 2 tests Expression. Both dimensions are reported on the certificate.

Examination Papers
Paper 01 1 hr 30 min
60 multiple-choice items. Section 1 (25 discrete grammar items) + Section 2 (35 comprehension items on 5 stimuli: poem, literary extract, expository extract, argumentative extract, visual extract)
29%
Paper 02 2 hr 40 min
Section A — Summary (25 marks) · Section B — Functional writing: email/letter/report/notice/article (30 marks) · Section C — Short story (optional, 25 marks) · Section D — Argumentative essay/speech/letter to editor (25 marks)
50%
Paper 031 SBA
Portfolio-based group project. Includes Plan of Investigation, 3+ sources (print, audio, visual), 3 Reflection entries, a Written Report (250–300 words), and a 3–5 min Oral Presentation in a genre of your choice
21%

Mastering the Summary
Paper 02 Section A is compulsory and worth 25 marks. Read the passage twice, identify the main points only, and write in your own words. Avoid lifting sentences directly. Aim for a single, coherent paragraph unless instructed otherwise.
🖊️
Short Story Essentials
A strong short story needs a clear opening hook, rising tension, a climax, and a resolution. Use vivid sensory detail, varied sentence structure, and consistent narrative voice. Plan for 5 minutes before you write — examiners reward structure.
🗣️
SBA Oral Presentation
Your 3–5 minute oral must be delivered predominantly in Caribbean Standard English. Choose a genre you're comfortable with (speech, drama, poetry, prose). Practise aloud multiple times. Always introduce your genre, sources, and the type of language used.

English B
Explores how writers, poets, and dramatists craft their work to influence our feelings and thoughts about life. English B is assessed across three literary profile dimensions: Drama, Poetry, and Prose Fiction. It requires close reading, critical analysis, and essay writing.
Drama
Drama — Profile Dimension 1
Shakespeare + Modern Drama · Paper 02 Section A
▼

Drama is studied as a discrete literary genre, with emphasis on elements unique to it. Students examine texts by Shakespeare alongside modern drama, exploring how the genre uses performance, spectacle, and stagecraft to convey theme and character.

Elements Unique to Drama
  • Performance as the main vehicle of communication
  • Stage directions and their effect on interpretation
  • Spectacle — how visual elements create meaning
  • Character development through dialogue and action
  • Atmosphere created through lighting, costuming, props
Critical Skills Tested
  • Knowledge and insight — relevance and accuracy of content
  • Organisation of response — structure and logical argument
  • Quality of language — clarity, grammar, and mechanics
  • Recognise the writer's craft in shaping character and behaviour
  • Identify moral assumptions and values in the text
Key Concepts to Study
Conflict & resolution Protagonist & antagonist Soliloquy & aside Dramatic irony Tragic flaw Subplot Themes & motifs Context & setting Shakespeare's language
Poetry
Poetry — Profile Dimension 2
20 Prescribed Poems · Paper 02 Section B
▼

Poetry is studied as a discrete literary genre, with a focus on elements specific to it: fixed forms, metre, rhythm, rhyme, economy of language, and the organic relationship between sound and sense. Students study a selection of 20 prescribed poems including Caribbean and other poetry in English.

Elements Unique to Poetry
  • Fixed forms — sonnet, ballad, ode, free verse, etc.
  • Metre, rhythm, and rhyme scheme
  • Economy of language — every word carries weight
  • Sound-sense relationship: onomatopoeia, assonance, alliteration
  • Figurative language giving levels of meaning
Exam Question Types
  • Type B Question 1 — Generic: compare two poems of your choice
  • Type B Question 2 — Named: two specific poems from the list
  • Answer ONE question from this section
  • Each question worth 35 marks
  • Must know at least 5–6 poems well for flexibility
Key Poetic Devices
Simile & metaphor Personification Alliteration Assonance Onomatopoeia Enjambment Caesura Tone & mood Speaker & persona Stanza structure Rhyme scheme (ABAB, etc.) Caribbean dialect in poetry
Prose
Prose Fiction — Profile Dimension 3
Novels & Short Stories · Paper 02 Section C
▼

Prose Fiction is the most expansive and accessible genre. Students study prescribed West Indian novels, other novels in English, and collections of short stories. The focus is on narrative technique, characterisation, structure, and how authors present human beings in social settings.

Elements of Prose Fiction
  • Narrative technique — first-person vs third-person narrators
  • Structure — unbroken narrative, chapters, multiple narratives
  • Narration vs description — knowing the difference
  • Characterisation — how characters are built and revealed
  • The presentation of humanity in a social setting
  • Themes — central ideas explored throughout the text
Exam Question Types
  • Type A Questions — Knowledge of ONE prescribed novel
  • Type B Questions — Compare two short stories from prescribed list
  • Answer ONE question from this section
  • Each question worth 35 marks
  • Prioritise West Indian texts for cultural context
Key Analytical Concepts
Point of view (POV) Reliable vs unreliable narrator Setting & atmosphere Conflict types Character motivation Foreshadowing Flashback Symbolism & motif Social commentary West Indian identity & culture

English B — Examination Structure
Papers, profiles, and mark breakdown
▼

English B is assessed across three profile dimensions: Drama, Poetry, and Prose Fiction. The certificate shows a grade for each. Responses require knowledge of text, insight, logical argument, and quality of written expression.

Examination Papers
Paper 01 2 hours
60 multiple-choice items. Each genre (Drama, Poetry, Prose) has 2 stimuli — 1 Caribbean and 1 other. 10 questions per stimulus = 20 questions per genre. All questions are compulsory.
29%
Paper 02 2 hr 10 min
3 sections — Drama (choose 1 of 2 questions, 35 marks each) · Poetry (choose 1 of 2 questions, 35 marks each) · Prose Fiction (choose 1 of 4 questions, 35 marks each). Answer 3 questions total, one per section.
50%
Paper 031 SBA (shared with A)
One single SBA portfolio counts for both English A and B. Includes Plan of Investigation, 3 sources, 3 Reflection entries, Written Report (~250–300 words), and 3–5 min Oral Presentation in a genre of choice.

SKILLS
Writing the Literary Essay
Critical skills tested in Paper 02 — all three genres
▼

Every Paper 02 question in English B is a 35-mark extended essay. Marks are awarded under two broad areas — Understanding (knowledge of text, insight, relevant examples) and Expression (structure, logical argument, quality of language). These are the skills to master.

Understanding — What to show
  • Describe, analyse, and synthesise information from the text
  • Respond to West Indian and other literatures with sensitivity
  • Make rational appraisals of values, judgements, and concepts
  • Recognise the writer's craft — technique in shaping language
  • Identify and distinguish moral assumptions in the text
  • Grasp how concepts and values are manifested in literature
Expression — How to write
  • Communicate opinions in well-structured, analytical responses
  • Use the vocabulary of literary criticism correctly
  • Produce balanced critical analyses — not just summaries
  • Structure responses with clear introduction, body, and conclusion
  • Write correctly in terms of grammar, punctuation, and spelling
Essay Writing Formula
Point → Evidence → Explain (PEE) Intro with clear argument/thesis Quotation + analysis Topic sentences per paragraph Linking words & cohesion Critical vocabulary Balanced argument Strong conclusion
🎭
Know Your Texts Deeply
Don't just read the texts — annotate them. Mark key quotes, note themes, identify characters' motivations, and track how the author builds tension or meaning. For poetry, read each poem at least 5 times, including aloud.
📝
Never Just Retell the Story
The examiners want analysis, not summary. Every paragraph should include a quotation or specific reference, followed by your explanation of what the writer is doing and why it's effective. Ask yourself: "How does the writer create this effect?"
🌍
Embrace Caribbean Literature
Caribbean texts are always included in Paper 01 and are a strong choice for Paper 02. Understand the social, historical, and cultural contexts of Caribbean writers — identity, colonialism, language, and community appear frequently as themes.
